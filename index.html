Worked example

This page walks one symptom, silent disagreement in meetings, through every step of the method.

01 · Symptom

What the business feels.

surface

The symptom is the thing your leadership team can describe at the kitchen table. It shows up in retros, in board prep, in the quiet "this isn't working" Slack from the executive who couldn't say it on the call.

Symptoms are not the problem. They are the signal that a behavioral pattern is operating beneath the surface, generating outputs the business can no longer ignore.

Some symptoms we hear most often:

  • Decisions don't stick. Two weeks later, the same conversation is happening again.
  • "I thought we agreed on X." Surprises in 1:1s, reviews, and retros.
  • Meetings that feel productive but produce nothing.
  • Recognition programs nobody trusts. Status moves quietly, not formally.
  • Talent flight in the people most likely to dissent.
"

Our meetings end with everyone nodding. Two days later, the Slack threads and water-cooler conversations make it obvious that nobody actually agreed. We're rebuilding the same decision four times.

What a leader hears, in their own words, before they call us. The symptom that opened this engagement: silent disagreement.

02 · Patterns

What's underneath.

name

Beneath every symptom is a behavioral pattern with a name. Patterns are observable: you can describe what people do, when they do it, and what they say afterward. The behavioral-science layer underneath each pattern explains why it persists, not as moral failing, but as mechanism.

Silent Disagreement
The release-valve pattern. Public agreement, private dissent.

Observable behaviors

  • People nod, stay silent, signal alignment in the room.
  • The leader closes with "any concerns?" and reads the silence as agreement.
  • Decisions are formally made on apparent consensus.
  • Within 48 hours, dissent appears in Slack threads, 1:1s, and water-cooler conversations.
  • Execution stalls or quietly forks; the same decision is rebuilt later as if for the first time.

The mechanism

Three behavioral forces compose the pattern. None of them are individual flaws. They are the predictable output of group dynamics under uneven status and asymmetric risk.

Loss aversion Herding (Asch conformity) Status threat Confirmation-seeking question shape

Connected patterns we often find alongside it

  • HiPPO: senior speaks first, group converges
  • Backchannel release valve: real talk in 1:1s and DMs
  • Meeting Theater: performance over decision
  • Decision Avoidance: meetings end without explicit decision moments
03 · Loops

Why it doesn't fix itself.

map

A pattern persists because it is held in place by reinforcing loops. Every loop has a polarity, a direction, and a leverage point. Mapping the loop is what turns "we should change the culture" into a specific intervention with a specific target.

All three loops are reinforcing. Left alone, they deepen. This is why willpower, awareness, and "let's all be more open" interventions don't work. The leverage point sits where two loops meet.

Phaseware in depth

The intervention, productized.

Bespoke. Branded as yours. Built around one specific pattern.

Phaseware is a small AI-powered application built specifically for your context, your language, your loop. It's not a SaaS product we sell to everyone. It is software designed for one client, one pattern, one engagement, and shipped under your brand.

  • Always bespoke. Two clients with the same symptom get two different Phasewares.
  • Always branded as yours. Your name, your visual identity, your voice.
  • Always AI-augmented. Trained on Glinda's accumulated knowledge of behavioral patterns plus your own data.
  • Three modes (disposable, leave-behind, or evolving), chosen to fit how the loop wants to be interrupted.

Disposable

One-time, single-purpose. A pre-mortem run before one critical decision. The app exists for a week, does its job, and retires.

Leave-behind

Permanent reinforcement. Lives in the rhythm of the team's week. Holds the new behavior in place when nobody is thinking about it.

Evolving

Starts as one shape, learns from how your people use it, and adapts. The pattern moves; the Phaseware moves with it.
