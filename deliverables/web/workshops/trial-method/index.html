The Problem

SMART kept the letters. It lost the system.

Peter Drucker built Management by Objectives for a slower economy — one where goals could be clear, aligned, revisited, and iteratively owned. Then we got SMART, which kept the letters and stripped out the governance. Specific. Measurable. Achievable. Relevant. Time-bound. Fine as a checklist. Useless as a system.

The missing half of MBO is what makes goals actually move: multiple strategies per goal, multiple tactics per strategy, multiple experiments per tactic — and a shared accountability for running the tests. SMART doesn't have it. OKRs half-have it. That is one tough Mother, Drucker.

The T.R.I.A.L. Method puts the missing pieces back. More manageable than MBO, more testable than SMART, built for a world where the right answer isn't knowable in advance.

How It Works

Two goals, walked down the stack.

One worked example teaches the method. Two worked examples teach the system. A revenue goal and a people-ops goal — same four tiers, completely different surface content.

Example A — Increase NPS

Goal
Increase NPS.
Strategy
Create a Client Advisory Board for guidance.
Tactic
Recruit clients with over 500 employees.
Experiments
  1. Ask directly in emails.
  2. Incentivize with perks.
  3. Send personalized gifts.

Example B — Reduce time to hire

Goal
Reduce time to hire.
Strategy
Remove steps from process.
Tactic
Decrease number of candidate interviews.
Experiments
  1. Turn 1:1s into group interviews.
  2. No more than 1 interviewer per team.
  3. Fewer qualifying interviews.

A goal is what you hope to achieve. A strategy is a high-level plan to achieve it. A tactic is an observable behavior that executes the strategy. An experiment is what tells you whether the tactic actually works.

Accountability isn't promising the goal. It's running the test.

The Session

Three moves in a room.

Step 1

Decompose.

Pick two or three live goals from the current cycle. Walk each one down the four tiers as a group — no breakout, no handoff. The strategy tier is where the real fight happens. Plan for it.

Step 2

Experiment design.

For each tactic, design three experiments. Not two. Not one with a fallback. Three. Constraint is the pedagogy.

Step 3

Commit and schedule.

Every experiment gets a metric, a comparison, a data threshold, and a plan for what's next. These are the four things an experiment needs. If any is missing, it isn't an experiment yet — it's a vibe.

Ways To Engage

Workshop, pull-through, coach-sulting.

Workshop

A facilitated session where your team builds its own T.R.I.A.L. stack — goal strata for 2–3 live priorities, tactic design, and an experiment queue you leave with. Half-day, most formats. Leadership or cross-functional planning group.

Pull-through

Recurring follow-up sessions for teams running their first T.R.I.A.L. cycle. Troubleshooting as tactics go live, experiments return data, and the stack gets revised. Monthly or fortnightly, over the first quarter of execution.

Coach-sulting

One-to-one or small-group coaching on goal decomposition — for leaders whose teams need more reps than a single workshop gives them. Open-ended; scoped per engagement.

What You Leave With

Not a talk. A thing your team built.

  • A shared vocabulary for decomposing goals. Your team leaves speaking the same four-tier language.
  • Worked goal strata for your current top 2–3 priorities. You didn't watch a talk. You built the thing.
  • An experiment queue with metrics, comparisons, and data thresholds already defined.
  • A governance cadence for revisiting experiments and revising tactics.
  • Clear visibility into where goals were stalling — usually at the strategy tier.
  • Accountability reframed as the ability to run the test — not the obligation to guarantee the outcome.
